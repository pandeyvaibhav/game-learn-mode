# Reading Inference 🎯

## What We're Learning
We are learning how to read between the lines. Authors don't always tell you everything directly — sometimes you have to use clues in the text plus what you already know to work out what is really happening or how a character feels.

## Key Words

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| inference | Working out something that isn't directly stated using clues |
| deduction | Using evidence from the text to reach a logical conclusion |
| evidence | The words or phrases in the text that support your idea |
| imply | When an author suggests something without saying it outright |

## Let's Explore! 🔍

### What Is Inference?
Inference is like being a detective. The author gives you clues, and you combine them with what you already know to figure things out. If a story says "Mia slammed the door and stomped upstairs," the author hasn't said Mia is angry — but you can infer it from her actions.

### Finding Evidence
Whenever you make an inference, you need to point to the evidence. Use the phrase: *"I think … because the text says …"* This shows you aren't just guessing — you have proof.

### Inferring Feelings, Motives and Settings
You can infer:
- **Feelings:** "His hands trembled as he opened the envelope." → He's nervous.
- **Motives:** "She hid the last biscuit behind her back." → She wants to keep it for herself.
- **Settings:** "Frost glittered on every surface and their breath hung in the air." → It's very cold outside.

## Try It Yourself! ✏️

**Question 1:** "Jake stared at the ground and didn't say a word." How might Jake be feeling?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>He might be feeling sad, guilty or upset — he is avoiding eye contact and staying silent.</details>

**Question 2:** What does "reading between the lines" mean?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>It means understanding something the author implies but doesn't directly state.</details>

**Question 3:** "The street was empty. Shutters were closed and newspapers blew across the road." What can you infer about this place?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>The place seems abandoned or deserted — there are no people and everything is closed up.</details>

**Question 4:** Why is it important to give evidence from the text when you infer?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>Evidence proves your inference is based on clues the author gave, not just a guess.</details>

**Question 5:** "She checked her watch for the third time and tapped her foot." What can you infer?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>She is impatient or anxious — she keeps checking the time and fidgeting.</details>

## Did You Know? 🌟
Good readers make inferences all the time without even realising it. Every time you predict what might happen next in a story, you're using inference!

- [ ] I can infer how a character feels from their actions and words
- [ ] I can use the phrase "I think … because the text says …"
