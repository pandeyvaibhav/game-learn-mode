# Stone Age to Iron Age — Year 3 History

## What We're Learning
You are going to travel back thousands of years to find out how early people in Britain lived. You will explore three periods — the **Stone Age**, the **Bronze Age** and the **Iron Age** — and see how tools, homes and ways of life changed over time.

## Key Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| Prehistory | The time before people could write things down. |
| Hunter-gatherer | A person who hunts animals and collects wild plants for food. |
| Neolithic | The "New Stone Age" when people began farming. |
| Bronze | A metal made by mixing copper and tin. |
| Iron | A strong metal used to make tools and weapons in the Iron Age. |
| Settlement | A place where a group of people lived together. |

### Stone Age (up to about 2500 BC)

- **Early Stone Age** — people were **hunter-gatherers**. They moved from place to place following animals.
- They used tools made from **flint** (a hard stone).
- **Skara Brae** in Orkney is a famous Stone Age village — it had stone beds and shelves!
- **Neolithic** people began **farming** — growing crops and keeping animals. They built permanent homes.
- They built **Stonehenge** — enormous stones arranged in a circle, possibly for ceremonies.

### Bronze Age (about 2500–800 BC)

- People discovered how to mix copper and tin to make **bronze** — harder than stone.
- Bronze was used for axes, daggers and jewellery.
- **Trade** grew — people swapped goods across long distances.
- Round barrows (burial mounds) were built for important people.

### Iron Age (about 800 BC–43 AD)

- People learned to make tools from **iron** — even stronger than bronze.
- They lived in **hillforts** — protected villages on hilltops.
- Farming improved: iron ploughs, better crops.
- **Celtic** art and culture flourished — beautiful shields and mirrors.
- Ended when the **Romans invaded** Britain in 43 AD.

# Stone Age to Iron Age — Year 3 History

## Big Question
How did life in Britain change from the Stone Age to the Iron Age — and what evidence tells us?

## What We're Learning
In this lesson you will travel back thousands of years to discover how the earliest people in Britain lived. You will explore how stone tools gave way to bronze and iron, how farming transformed society, and why Stonehenge remains one of the world’s greatest mysteries.

## Key History Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| prehistoric | the time before writing was invented — we rely on artefacts, not written records |
| Stone Age | the long period when people made tools from stone (divided into Old, Middle and New) |
| hunter-gatherer | a person who survives by hunting animals and collecting wild plants |
| Neolithic | the "New Stone Age" — when farming began in Britain (about 4000 BC) |
| Bronze Age | the period when people learned to mix tin and copper to make bronze tools (about 2500 BC) |
| Iron Age | the period when people made stronger tools from iron (about 800 BC) |
| Celts | the people who lived in Britain during the Iron Age |
| artefact | an object made by humans that tells us about the past |
| Stonehenge | a famous stone circle in Wiltshire, built over many centuries in the Neolithic and Bronze Ages |

## The History Explained

### The Stone Age (around 800,000 BC – 2500 BC)

The earliest people in Britain were **hunter-gatherers**. They moved from place to place following animals and finding berries, nuts and roots.

- **Tools:** Made by chipping pieces of flint to create sharp edges — hand axes, scrapers, arrowheads.
- **Shelter:** Caves and simple shelters made from branches and animal skins.
- **Art:** Cave paintings show us what animals they hunted.

#### The Farming Revolution (Neolithic — from about 4000 BC)
A huge change happened: people learned to **farm**! They planted crops (wheat, barley) and kept animals (cattle, sheep). This meant they no longer had to move around — they built permanent settlements.

### The Bronze Age (about 2500 BC – 800 BC)
People discovered that mixing **copper** and **tin** makes a much harder metal called **bronze**. Bronze tools and weapons were sharper and lasted longer than stone.

- **Stonehenge** was completed during this period. The huge stones were transported from Wales — over 150 miles away!
- Trade grew because bronze could not be made everywhere — people needed to swap materials.

### The Iron Age (about 800 BC – AD 43)
**Iron** is even stronger than bronze. Iron tools made farming more effective, and iron weapons made warriors more powerful.

- The **Celts** built large **hillforts** for protection — you can still see the earthworks at places like Maiden Castle in Dorset.
- Celtic society had chiefs, druids (priests), and skilled craftspeople who made beautiful jewellery and decorated shields.
- The Iron Age ended when the **Romans** invaded Britain in AD 43.

### How do we know about prehistoric Britain?
Since there were no written records, historians and archaeologists use **artefacts** and **evidence** from the ground:
- Bones and teeth tell us what people ate.
- Flint tools show us their technology.
- Burial sites reveal their beliefs.
- Stonehenge itself is evidence of incredible organisation and engineering.

## History in the Real World
You can visit **Stonehenge** in Wiltshire — scientists are still debating exactly how and why it was built! Many Iron Age hillforts, like **Maiden Castle**, are open to explore. The tools and jewellery discovered by archaeologists are displayed in museums such as the British Museum in London.
