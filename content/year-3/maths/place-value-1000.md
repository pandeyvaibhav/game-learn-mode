# Place Value to 1000 — Year 3 Maths

## What We're Learning
You are going to learn how numbers up to 1000 are made from hundreds, tens and ones. You will see how each digit has its own value depending on where it sits, and you will learn how to round numbers to the nearest 10 or 100.

## Key Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| Hundreds | Groups of 100 — the digit in the hundreds column tells you how many. |
| Tens | Groups of 10 — the digit in the tens column tells you how many. |
| Ones | Single units — the digit in the ones column tells you how many. |
| Place value | The value a digit has because of its position in a number. |
| Partition | Breaking a number into hundreds, tens and ones. |
| Rounding | Finding the nearest 10 or 100 to make a number simpler. |

## Let's Explore

Every three-digit number is built from **hundreds**, **tens** and **ones**. Think of it like boxes — a big box holds 100, a medium box holds 10 and a small box holds 1.

### Example 1 — Partitioning 365

```
Hundreds | Tens | Ones
    3    |   6  |   5
```

- The **3** means **300**
- The **6** means **60**
- The **5** means **5**
- 365 = 300 + 60 + 5

### Example 2 — Placing numbers on a number line

Where does **470** sit?

```
0----100----200----300----400----500----600----700----800----900----1000
                                  ^
                                 470
```

470 is between 400 and 500, closer to 500.

### Example 3 — Rounding to the nearest 10

To round **247**, look at the ones digit. It is **7** (5 or more → round up).

```
240---245---247---250
              ^     ↑
             247   rounds to 250
```

247 rounded to the nearest 10 is **250**.

### Example 4 — Rounding to the nearest 100

To round **631**, look at the tens digit. It is **3** (less than 5 → round down).

631 rounded to the nearest 100 is **600**.

## Did You Know? 🧮
The ancient Romans did not use place value at all! They wrote numbers with letters like I, V, X, C and M. Our system came from India and was spread by Arab mathematicians.

# Place Value to 1000 — Year 3 Maths

## What We're Learning
In this lesson you will explore how numbers up to 1000 are built. You will discover how **hundreds**, **tens** and **ones** give each digit its value, and you will practise comparing and rounding 3-digit numbers.

## Key Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| place value | the value of a digit based on its position in a number |
| hundreds | the column worth groups of 100 |
| tens | the column worth groups of 10 |
| ones | the column worth single units |
| compare | deciding which number is greater or smaller using < > = |
| round | finding the nearest 10 or 100 to estimate a number |
| partition | breaking a number into hundreds, tens and ones |

## Let's Explore

Every digit in a number has a different value depending on its **position**. Look at **256**:

```
Hundreds | Tens | Ones
    2    |   5  |   6
  200    +  50  +   6  = 256
```

The **2** is worth 200 because it sits in the hundreds column.
The **5** is worth 50 because it sits in the tens column.
The **6** is worth 6 because it sits in the ones column.

This is called **partitioning**: 256 = 200 + 50 + 6.

### Example 1 — Partition the number 743

```
Hundreds | Tens | Ones
    7    |   4  |   3
```

- The **7** is worth **700**
- The **4** is worth **40**
- The **3** is worth **3**
- 743 = 700 + 40 + 3 ✓

### Example 2 — Comparing 395 and 412

1. Compare the hundreds first: **3** hundreds vs **4** hundreds. 4 hundreds is more.
2. No need to check tens or ones — the hundreds already tell us.
3. So **412 > 395**.

```
390---395---400------412------420
      ↑               ↑
    smaller          bigger
```

### Example 3 — Rounding 456 to the nearest 10 and nearest 100

**Nearest 10:** 456 is between 450 and 460. The ones digit is 6 (5 or more means round up). **456 ≈ 460**

**Nearest 100:** 456 is between 400 and 500. The tens digit is 5 (5 or more means round up). **456 ≈ 500**

```
400------450---456------500
                ↑
      closer to 500 → rounds to 500
```

## Did You Know? 🧮
It takes about 17 minutes to count aloud from 1 to 1000 — roughly the length of a cartoon episode! The ancient Romans used the letter **M** for 1000. The longest number word in English under 1000 is "seven hundred and seventy-seven".
