# Light and Shadows — Year 3 Science

## Big Question
Why do shadows change shape and size during the day?

## What We're Learning
In this lesson you will explore where **light** comes from, how **shadows** form, and why reflections happen. You will also discover how people used shadows to tell the time before clocks were invented!

## Key Science Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| light source | something that makes its own light (e.g. the Sun, a torch, a candle) |
| shadow | a dark area made when an object blocks light |
| opaque | does not let light through — makes a dark shadow (e.g. wood, metal) |
| translucent | lets some light through — makes a faint shadow (e.g. frosted glass) |
| transparent | lets all light through — makes no shadow (e.g. clear glass) |
| reflect | when light bounces off a surface |
| sundial | an ancient clock that uses the position of a shadow to tell the time |

## The Science Explained

**Light travels in straight lines.** When something gets in the way, light cannot bend around it — so a dark area (a **shadow**) appears on the other side.

The shadow is always on the opposite side of the object from the light source:

```
  ☀️ LIGHT ───→  [ OBJECT ]  ───→ ████ SHADOW
```

**Shadow size depends on position.** When the object is close to the light source, the shadow is bigger. When it is further away, the shadow is smaller.

**During the day** the Sun moves across the sky (it looks like it moves, but really the Earth is rotating). In the morning, shadows are long because the Sun is low. At midday, shadows are short. In the evening, shadows are long again — but pointing the opposite direction!

### Reflections

Light bounces off smooth, shiny surfaces like mirrors and water. That is why you can see yourself in a mirror — the light bouncing off you reflects back to your eyes.

Not all surfaces reflect well. Rough surfaces scatter light in all directions, so you cannot see a clear reflection.

## Explore It! — Investigation Idea
**Question:** How does the distance between a torch and an object change the shadow size?
**You need:** A torch, a small toy figure, a plain wall, a ruler, a piece of paper
**What to do:**
1. Place the toy 10 cm from the wall.
2. Shine the torch from 50 cm away. Draw around the shadow on the paper.
3. Move the torch to 30 cm away. Draw the new shadow.
4. Move the torch to 10 cm away. Draw again.
**What to notice:** Which distance made the biggest shadow?
**Why it happens:** When the light source is closer to the object, the light rays spread more around it, creating a larger shadow.

## Quick Quiz
1. Name two light sources. → *The Sun and a torch (or candle, lamp, etc.)*
2. What kind of material lets no light through? → *Opaque*
3. When are shadows shortest during the day? → *At midday (when the Sun is highest)*
4. What does "reflect" mean? → *When light bounces off a surface*
5. What ancient device uses shadows to tell the time? → *A sundial*

## Science in the Real World
Shadow puppetry is an ancient art form from Southeast Asia — performers hold cut-out figures between a lamp and a screen to tell stories! Architects also think carefully about shadows when designing buildings, making sure rooms get enough sunlight. And of course, without shadows we would never have invented the sundial — one of the earliest ways humans told the time.
