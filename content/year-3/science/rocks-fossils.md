# Rocks and Fossils — Year 3 Science

## Big Question
How are different types of rock formed, and how do fossils end up trapped inside them?

## What We're Learning
In this lesson you will discover the three types of rock — **igneous**, **sedimentary**, and **metamorphic** — and find out how fossils form over millions of years. Rocks tell the story of our planet!

## Key Science Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| igneous | rock made from cooled magma or lava (e.g. granite, basalt) |
| sedimentary | rock made from layers of sediment pressed together over time (e.g. limestone, sandstone) |
| metamorphic | rock that has been changed by heat and pressure (e.g. marble, slate) |
| fossil | the preserved remains or trace of a living thing from long ago |
| magma | hot melted rock beneath the Earth's surface |
| lava | magma that reaches the Earth's surface through a volcano |
| sediment | tiny pieces of rock, sand, or mud carried by water or wind |

## The Science Explained

**Igneous rock** forms when hot magma from deep inside the Earth cools and hardens. If it cools slowly underground, it makes large crystals (like **granite**). If it cools quickly above ground from lava, it makes small crystals (like **basalt**).

**Sedimentary rock** forms when tiny bits of rock, shell, and sand settle in layers — usually at the bottom of rivers, lakes, or the sea. Over millions of years, the weight of new layers squashes and cements the old layers together. This is where most **fossils** are found!

**Metamorphic rock** starts as igneous or sedimentary rock, but intense heat and pressure deep underground change it into something new. Limestone becomes **marble**, and mudstone becomes **slate**.

### How fossils form

1. An animal or plant dies and falls to the ground or seabed.
2. Layers of sediment cover it before it rots away completely.
3. Over millions of years, minerals seep into the remains and turn them to stone.
4. Earth movements push the fossil closer to the surface.
5. Erosion or digging finally reveals the fossil!

## Explore It! — Investigation Idea
**Question:** Which types of rock absorb the most water?
**You need:** 3 different rocks (e.g. chalk, granite, slate), a bowl of water, kitchen scales, paper towels
**What to do:**
1. Weigh each rock and write down its mass.
2. Place all three rocks in the bowl of water for 5 minutes.
3. Take them out, pat dry with a paper towel, and weigh again.
**What to notice:** Which rock gained the most weight?
**Why it happens:** Sedimentary rocks often have tiny gaps (pores) that soak up water. Igneous and metamorphic rocks are usually denser with fewer gaps.

## Quick Quiz
1. What type of rock is granite? → *Igneous*
2. Which type of rock is most likely to contain fossils? → *Sedimentary*
3. What is lava? → *Magma that has reached the Earth's surface*
4. What two forces change rock into metamorphic rock? → *Heat and pressure*
5. Name one example of a metamorphic rock. → *Marble (or slate)*

## Science in the Real World
The cliffs of Dover in England are made of white **chalk** — a sedimentary rock made from the tiny shells of sea creatures that lived millions of years ago. Builders use **granite** for strong kitchen worktops and **slate** for roof tiles because they are hard-wearing. Every time you see a stone building, you are looking at millions of years of Earth's history!
