# Forces and Magnets — Year 3 Science

## Big Question
What makes things move, stop, or change direction — and why do magnets stick to some things but not others?

## What We're Learning
In this lesson you will learn about **forces** — pushes and pulls that make things move. You will discover **friction** and explore the invisible force of **magnetism**, including why magnets attract some materials and repel each other.

## Key Science Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| force | a push or a pull that can make something move, stop, or change direction |
| friction | a force that slows things down when two surfaces rub together |
| magnet | an object that produces an invisible force that attracts iron and steel |
| attract | to pull towards (opposite poles of a magnet attract) |
| repel | to push away (same poles of a magnet repel) |
| magnetic | a material that is attracted to a magnet (iron, steel, nickel, cobalt) |
| pole | the two ends of a magnet — north (N) and south (S) |

## The Science Explained

**Forces are everywhere.** Every time you kick a ball, open a door, or pick up a book, you are using a force. Forces can:
- Make something **start moving**
- Make something **stop**
- Make something **change direction**
- Make something **change shape**

### Friction

**Friction** is a force that happens when two surfaces rub against each other. It **slows things down**.

- A ball rolling on **grass** stops quickly — lots of friction.
- A ball rolling on **ice** keeps going — very little friction.

Friction can be useful (shoe grip on the ground) or a nuisance (rubbing your hands makes them hot!).

### Magnets

Magnets have two **poles**: **north (N)** and **south (S)**.

```
  [N]─────────[S]     [N]─────────[S]
       Magnet 1             Magnet 2

  N ←→ S  = ATTRACT (pull together)
  N ←→ N  = REPEL (push apart)
  S ←→ S  = REPEL (push apart)
```

**Rule:** Opposite poles attract. Same poles repel.

Only certain materials are magnetic: **iron**, **steel**, **nickel**, and **cobalt**. Wood, plastic, glass, and aluminium are NOT magnetic.

## Explore It! — Investigation Idea
**Question:** Which surfaces produce the most friction?
**You need:** A toy car, a ramp (a propped-up book), a ruler, different surfaces (carpet, foil, sandpaper, smooth table)
**What to do:**
1. Place each surface at the bottom of the ramp.
2. Let the car roll down from the same starting point each time.
3. Measure how far the car travels on each surface.
**What to notice:** On which surface did the car travel the shortest distance?
**Why it happens:** Rough surfaces create more friction, slowing the car down faster.

## Quick Quiz
1. Name two types of force. → *Push and pull*
2. What force slows a rolling ball? → *Friction*
3. What happens when two north poles of magnets are brought together? → *They repel (push apart)*
4. Name a magnetic material. → *Iron (or steel, nickel, cobalt)*
5. Is aluminium magnetic? → *No*

## Science in the Real World
Magnets are used in everyday life more than you might think! **Fridge magnets** hold notes in place, **compasses** use a magnetic needle to find north, and the **maglev trains** in Japan and China use powerful magnets to float above the track — reaching speeds over 600 km/h with almost no friction!
