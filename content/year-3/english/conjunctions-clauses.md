# Conjunctions and Clauses — Year 3 English

## What We're Learning
You are going to learn how to join ideas together using **conjunctions**. You will discover the difference between a **main clause** and a **subordinate clause**, and use words like *when*, *because*, *if* and *although* to make your sentences longer and more interesting.

## Key Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| Conjunction | A word that joins two parts of a sentence. |
| Main clause | A group of words that makes sense on its own. |
| Subordinate clause | A group of words that adds extra information but cannot stand alone. |
| Subordinating conjunction | A conjunction that starts a subordinate clause (when, because, if, although). |
| Co-ordinating conjunction | A conjunction that joins two equal parts (and, but, or, so, yet). |

## Let's Explore

### Co-ordinating conjunctions

These join two main clauses — both parts make sense alone:

- I like apples **and** I like oranges.
- She ran fast **but** she missed the bus.
- We could paint **or** we could draw.

Remember **FANBOYS**: **F**or, **A**nd, **N**or, **B**ut, **O**r, **Y**et, **S**o.

### Subordinating conjunctions

These introduce a subordinate clause — a part that needs the main clause:

- I put on my coat **because** it was cold.
- **When** the bell rang, everyone lined up.
- **If** it rains, we will stay inside.
- She smiled **although** she was tired.

The subordinate clause (underlined below) cannot stand alone:

> I stayed inside **because it was raining**.

"Because it was raining" does not make sense by itself — it needs the main clause "I stayed inside".

### Clause spotting

| Sentence | Main clause | Subordinate clause |
|---|---|---|
| We cheered when our team scored. | We cheered | when our team scored |
| Although it was dark, the path was clear. | the path was clear | Although it was dark |
| She whispered because the baby was sleeping. | She whispered | because the baby was sleeping |

**Tip:** When the subordinate clause comes first, put a comma after it.

## Did You Know? 📝
The word "conjunction" comes from Latin *conjungere* meaning "to join together". That is exactly what conjunctions do — they join parts of a sentence!

# Conjunctions and Clauses — Year 3 English

## What We're Learning
You are going to learn how to use **subordinating conjunctions** — special joining words like **because**, **although**, and **when** — to build longer, more varied sentences. You will also discover the difference between a **main clause** and a **subordinate clause**.

## Key Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| Conjunction | A word that joins two parts of a sentence together |
| Subordinating conjunction | A conjunction that starts a less important (subordinate) clause — e.g. because, although, when, if, while |
| Main clause | The part of a sentence that makes sense on its own |
| Subordinate clause | A part that adds extra information but cannot stand alone |
| Clause | A group of words that contains a subject and a verb |

## Let's Learn Together

### What Is a Clause?

A **clause** has a subject (who or what) and a verb (doing word):
- **The cat sat** on the mat. ← one clause
- **The cat sat** on the mat **because it was tired**. ← two clauses

### Main Clause vs Subordinate Clause

| Part | Example | Can it stand alone? |
|---|---|---|
| Main clause | The cat sat on the mat | Yes ✓ |
| Subordinate clause | because it was tired | No ✗ |

The subordinate clause adds a reason, time, or condition — but it needs the main clause to make full sense.

### Common Subordinating Conjunctions

| Conjunction | What it shows |
|---|---|
| because | reason |
| although / even though | contrast (something surprising) |
| when / while / as | time |
| if | condition |
| until | time limit |

### Sentences With Subordinating Conjunctions

**Main clause first:**
> I wore my coat **because** it was cold.

**Subordinate clause first** (notice the comma!):
> **Although** it was sunny, I still wore a jumper.

**Rule:** When the subordinate clause comes **first**, put a **comma** before the main clause.
