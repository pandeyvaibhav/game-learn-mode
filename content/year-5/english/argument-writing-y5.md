# Argument Writing 🎯

## What We're Learning
We're learning how to write a balanced argument — a piece of writing that looks at both sides of a debate before reaching a conclusion. You'll use evidence, connectives and counter-arguments to make your writing fair, logical and persuasive.

## Key Words

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| balanced argument | A text that presents both sides of an issue fairly |
| connective | A word or phrase that links ideas, e.g. "however," "on the other hand" |
| evidence | Facts, statistics or examples that support a point |
| counter-argument | A point made against the opposing view |

## Let's Explore! 🔍

### What Is a Balanced Argument?
A balanced argument explores a question or issue by presenting reasons for and against. For example: "Should school uniforms be compulsory?" You give arguments on both sides and then state your own opinion in the conclusion. The goal is to show you have considered different viewpoints, not just your own.

### Using Connectives and Structure
A clear structure helps the reader follow your reasoning. Start with an introduction that states the issue. Then write one or two paragraphs for each side. Use connectives to signal which side you're presenting: "Firstly," "In addition," "Furthermore" for one side; "However," "On the other hand," "Nevertheless" for the opposing side. End with a conclusion giving your personal viewpoint.

### Evidence and Counter-Arguments
Strong arguments are backed by evidence — facts, statistics, expert opinions or real examples. For instance, "According to a survey, 70% of pupils said uniforms saved time in the morning." A counter-argument responds to the other side: "Although some argue uniforms limit expression, supporters say they reduce peer pressure." This shows critical thinking.

## Try It Yourself! ✏️

**Question 1:** What is the difference between a balanced argument and a persuasive text?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>A balanced argument presents both sides fairly before giving a conclusion. A persuasive text only argues one side to convince the reader.</details>

**Question 2:** Give two connectives you could use to introduce an opposing point.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>"However," "On the other hand," "Nevertheless," "Despite this."</details>

**Question 3:** Why is evidence important in argument writing?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>Evidence makes your points stronger and more convincing because they are based on facts, not just opinions.</details>

**Question 4:** Write a counter-argument for: "Homework takes up children's free time."
<details><summary>Answer</summary>Example: "However, supporters of homework argue that it helps children practise skills and develop independent study habits."</details>

**Question 5:** Where in a balanced argument should you give your own opinion?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>In the conclusion, after you have presented both sides of the argument.</details>

## Did You Know? 🌟
The art of arguing well has been studied for over 2,000 years! The ancient Greeks called it "rhetoric" and taught it as one of the most important subjects in school.
