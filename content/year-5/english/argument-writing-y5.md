# Argument Writing — Year 5 English

## Big Question
How do we write a balanced argument that presents both sides fairly?

## What We're Learning
In this lesson you will learn to write a **balanced argument** with points **for** and **against**, using **formal connectives** and a clear **conclusion**.

## Key English Words
| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| balanced argument | a piece of writing that presents both sides of a debate |
| for | reasons supporting an idea |
| against | reasons opposing an idea |
| connective | a word or phrase that links ideas (e.g. however, on the other hand) |
| conclusion | a final paragraph summing up and giving your opinion |

## The English Explained

### Structure
1. **Introduction** — state the issue as a question or statement.
2. **Arguments FOR** — give 2–3 reasons with evidence.
3. **Arguments AGAINST** — give 2–3 reasons with evidence.
4. **Conclusion** — weigh up both sides and give your own opinion.

### Useful Connectives
| For | Against | Concluding |
|---|---|---|
| Firstly… | However… | In conclusion… |
| Furthermore… | On the other hand… | Having considered both sides… |
| In addition… | Nevertheless… | On balance, I believe… |

### Tips
- Use a **formal tone** — no slang or exclamation marks.
- Support each point with **evidence or examples**.
- Be **fair** — don't make one side seem silly.
- End with your **own opinion**, clearly stated.

### Example Opening
> **Should children be allowed to use smartphones in school?** This is a topic that many people have strong views about. In this essay, I will explore both sides of the argument before giving my own opinion.

## Quick Quiz
1. What is a balanced argument? → *Writing that presents both sides of a debate*
2. Name a connective for introducing the opposing view. → *However / On the other hand / Nevertheless*
3. What should the conclusion include? → *A summary of both sides and your own opinion*
4. What tone should you use? → *Formal*

## English in the Real World
Balanced arguments appear in newspaper editorials, parliamentary debates, and academic essays. Lawyers present both sides in court. Being able to argue fairly is a key life skill.

## Learning Checklist
- [ ] I can structure a balanced argument (for, against, conclusion)
- [ ] I can use formal connectives to link points
- [ ] I can write a clear conclusion with my own opinion
