# Victorian Britain 🎯

## What We're Learning
We're stepping into Victorian Britain — the era when Queen Victoria sat on the throne (1837–1901). You'll explore how the Industrial Revolution transformed the country, learn about the harsh reality of child labour, and discover the incredible inventions that changed daily life forever.

## Key Words

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Victorian | Relating to the reign of Queen Victoria, 1837–1901 |
| Industrial Revolution | The period of rapid change when factories and machines replaced hand-made production |
| child labour | The use of children as workers, often in dangerous conditions |
| invention | A new device or process created for the first time |

## Let's Explore! 🔍

### Queen Victoria and the Era
Queen Victoria became queen at just 18 years old and reigned for 63 years — the longest reign until Queen Elizabeth II. The Victorian era saw Britain become the most powerful nation in the world, with a vast empire. Society was sharply divided between the wealthy and the poor. Rich Victorians lived in grand houses, while many poor families were crammed into tiny, unhealthy slums.

### The Industrial Revolution and Child Labour
The Industrial Revolution brought factories, railways and mass production. Cities like Manchester, Birmingham and Leeds grew rapidly as people moved from the countryside to find work. Tragically, many children as young as five worked in factories, mines and as chimney sweeps. The hours were long, the work was dangerous, and pay was tiny. Reformers like Lord Shaftesbury campaigned to pass laws protecting children, including the Factory Acts that limited working hours and set minimum ages.

### Victorian Inventions
The Victorians were great inventors. The railway network expanded across Britain, connecting cities and towns. Alexander Graham Bell demonstrated the telephone, and Thomas Edison developed practical electric lighting. Photography became widespread, the postage stamp was introduced (the Penny Black in 1840), and Joseph Bazalgette designed London's sewer system to tackle deadly diseases like cholera. These inventions laid the foundations for modern life.

## Did You Know? 🌟
Victorian children didn't have a free education until 1880, when a law was passed making school compulsory for all children aged 5 to 10. Before that, many poor children never went to school at all!
